# Cold Stress

## Overview

Cold stress occurs when poultry lose body heat faster than they can produce it. Chicks are especially vulnerable because their temperature control is not fully developed in early life. Cold stress reduces growth, weakens immunity, and increases mortality when brooding and house management are poor. Even in warm regions, night temperature drops and wet draughty housing can trigger significant flock losses. Cold stress problems are often worse after rain, during power interruptions, or when ventilation is mismanaged and damp air accumulates inside the house.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Cold stress causes birds to use more feed energy for heat production instead of growth or egg output. Chilled birds become weak, crowd together, and can suffocate in severe cases. Persistent cold stress increases respiratory disease risk because damp cold air and poor ventilation damage airway defences. Better cold management improves feed efficiency, survival, and flock uniformity.

### Causes

- Low ambient temperature, especially at night
- Cold draughts at bird level from poor curtain control
- Wet litter and high house humidity
- Inadequate brooder heat for chick age
- Uneven heat distribution inside the house

### Risk Factors

- Day-old and young chicks in first two weeks
- High altitude or rainy season production areas
- Houses with poor insulation or damaged walls
- Frequent power cuts affecting brooder heaters
- Overcrowding that worsens moisture and air quality

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Huddling, shivering, and loud distress chirping in chicks
- Slow growth and poor feed conversion
- Uneven body weight and delayed flock maturity
- Increased respiratory signs and secondary infections
- Higher early chick mortality and culling rate

### Prevention

Preheat houses before chick placement and maintain stable brooder temperature by age. Eliminate cold draughts while preserving minimum fresh-air exchange. Keep litter dry and replace wet patches quickly to reduce conductive heat loss. Use reliable temperature monitoring and backup plans for power interruption during cold nights.

### Monitoring

Monitor birds and environment continuously, not temperature alone.

- Measure brooder and floor temperature at multiple points.
- Observe chick distribution: even spread indicates acceptable comfort.
- Check for corner crowding, piling, or shivering behaviour.
- Monitor litter dryness and condensation on walls or roof.
- Track early mortality, daily gain, and flock uniformity trends.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Preheat the house before chick arrival and verify uniform floor warmth.
2. Adjust brooder temperature daily according to chick age and behaviour.
3. Prevent direct draughts while allowing minimum fresh-air exchange.
4. Keep litter dry and add clean dry bedding when needed.
5. Check heater function and fuel or power backup every day.
6. Avoid overcrowding, especially during cold wet weather.
7. Review growth and mortality records each week for early correction.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Take immediate action when chicks pile tightly under heat sources, distress chirping increases, or birds are found weak and cold in house corners. Emergency response is also needed when sudden night mortality occurs or when heater failure happens during low temperature periods. Fast correction can prevent large losses within a few hours.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] House is preheated before chick placement
- [ ] Brooder temperature matches chick age target
- [ ] No direct cold draught at bird level
- [ ] Litter is dry and free of large wet patches
- [ ] Heater, fuel, and backup power are ready
- [ ] Chick distribution is even across the brooder area
- [ ] Early mortality and growth are checked daily
