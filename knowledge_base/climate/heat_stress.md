# Heat Stress

## Overview

Heat stress occurs when poultry cannot remove enough body heat to stay in a safe temperature range. Chickens are highly sensitive to high ambient temperature and humidity because they do not sweat and rely on breathing to lose heat. Heat stress reduces growth, lowers egg production, and can cause sudden flock mortality during hot periods. Early prevention and fast response are essential in tropical and semi-arid poultry systems. Heat stress risk is highest in late afternoon and during heat waves, especially when ventilation, shade, and water systems are inadequate.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Heat stress causes immediate welfare problems and major economic losses. Birds under heat stress eat less feed, convert feed poorly, and produce fewer eggs with weaker shells. Prolonged heat exposure suppresses immunity and increases susceptibility to respiratory and enteric disease. Severe heat waves can cause rapid flock collapse, especially in crowded houses with poor airflow.

### Causes

- High daytime house temperature above flock tolerance
- High humidity that limits evaporative cooling from panting
- Inadequate ventilation during hot afternoons
- Low water flow or poor access to cool drinking water
- Overcrowding that increases metabolic heat load

### Risk Factors

- Broilers in late grow-out with high body weight
- Layers in peak production with high metabolic demand
- Dark roofing without insulation or shade
- Long transport or handling during midday heat
- Power failure in mechanically ventilated houses

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Panting, wing spreading, and lethargy
- Reduced feed intake with increased water intake
- Drop in egg production and shell quality
- Poor weight gain and reduced carcass quality in broilers
- Increased mortality, especially in heavy birds

### Prevention

Reduce heat load before peak temperature each day. Improve airflow, provide reliable cool water, and lower stocking density during hot months if needed. Use shade, reflective roofing, or local insulation methods to reduce radiant heat. Schedule handling, vaccination, and transport during cooler morning or evening hours.

### Monitoring

Monitor temperature, humidity, and bird behaviour continuously during hot weather.

- Record house temperature and humidity at least three times daily.
- Watch for early panting and wing spreading in multiple pens.
- Check drinker function and water temperature during peak heat.
- Compare daily feed intake and egg count with normal baseline.
- Track hourly mortality during heat waves for rapid intervention.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Increase ventilation before midday heat starts.
2. Ensure all drinkers are clean, working, and easy to reach.
3. Add temporary shade over roofs or side walls where possible.
4. Reduce handling and flock movement during hot hours.
5. Provide cooler water and check flow rate frequently.
6. Reduce house density in high-risk seasons.
7. Review daily flock records to detect heat effects early.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Treat as an emergency when many birds show continuous open-mouth panting, collapse, or inability to stand. Immediate action is required if mortality rises rapidly within hours, especially in heavy broilers. Emergency response is also needed when power outages stop fans during peak heat, because fatal losses can occur quickly.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Temperature and humidity are recorded every day
- [ ] Ventilation is increased before afternoon heat peaks
- [ ] Cool, clean drinking water is continuously available
- [ ] Bird crowding and handling are minimized during hot periods
- [ ] Shade and roof heat reduction measures are in place
- [ ] Backup power or manual ventilation plan is ready
- [ ] Heat-related mortality and egg drop are tracked daily
