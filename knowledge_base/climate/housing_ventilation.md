# Housing Ventilation

## Overview

Housing ventilation is the controlled movement of fresh air through a poultry house to remove heat, moisture, dust, and harmful gases. Good ventilation keeps birds comfortable, supports normal breathing, and protects feed conversion and egg production. Poor ventilation causes wet litter, high ammonia, and respiratory stress, which increase disease risk and farm losses. In low-resource settings, simple airflow management can improve flock health even without expensive equipment. Ventilation also stabilizes house temperature across day and night, which helps reduce both heat stress and cold stress events.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Ventilation directly affects survival, growth rate, egg yield, and medicine costs. Birds perform best when house air stays fresh and litter stays dry. Good airflow reduces ammonia exposure, lowers pathogen pressure, and improves vaccine response by reducing chronic stress. Ventilation is also a core biosecurity support measure because cleaner air lowers respiratory infection pressure.

### Causes of Poor Ventilation

- Inlets or windows blocked by plastic, dust, or damaged curtains
- Fan failure or weak fan capacity for flock size
- Overcrowding beyond house airflow capacity
- Poor house orientation against prevailing wind
- Inadequate maintenance of vents, belts, or power supply

### Risk Factors

- High stocking density during humid seasons
- Deep litter with high moisture content
- Large daily temperature swings without airflow adjustment
- Houses with limited side openings
- Long periods of electricity interruption where fans are required

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Coughing, sneezing, open-mouth breathing, and reduced flock activity
- Wet litter leading to footpad lesions, breast blisters, and dirty feathers
- Eye irritation and poor feed intake from high ammonia
- Slower growth in broilers and lower egg output in layers
- Higher mortality during heat stress or cold stress events

### Prevention

Maintain minimum airflow every day, even in cool weather. Keep inlets clean and ensure air enters above bird level before mixing. Remove wet litter quickly and repair leaks from roofs or drinker lines. Match bird numbers to house capacity and adjust curtains, vents, and fan use by weather conditions.

### Monitoring

Monitor house conditions at least three times daily and during major weather changes.

- Smell for ammonia at bird head height; strong smell indicates poor airflow.
- Check litter moisture by hand squeeze; litter should not clump into wet balls.
- Observe bird distribution; crowding in corners often signals airflow problems.
- Record temperature and humidity in morning, afternoon, and night.
- Track feed intake, water intake, and mortality for early detection.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Open side curtains or inlets early to bring in fresh air before heat builds.
2. Keep airflow continuous at night to prevent moisture build-up.
3. Clean fan blades, screens, and inlets every week.
4. Reduce stocking density if litter remains wet despite correction.
5. Fix water leaks immediately to prevent ammonia rise.
6. Train workers to identify bird signs of poor air quality.
7. Keep daily ventilation records and adjust settings by weather.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Act immediately when birds show open-mouth breathing, severe crowding near doors, rapid rise in house smell, or sudden mortality increase during hot or humid periods. Emergency response is also needed when litter turns wet across large areas within one day, because this can trigger respiratory outbreaks and foot lesions quickly.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Inlets and vents are open and unobstructed
- [ ] Air movement is present at bird level without strong draughts
- [ ] House ammonia smell is low
- [ ] Litter remains mostly dry across all sections
- [ ] Fans and power backup are functional where installed
- [ ] Bird distribution is even throughout the house
- [ ] Ventilation, weather, and flock observations are recorded daily
