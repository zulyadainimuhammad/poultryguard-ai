# Avian Influenza

## Overview

Avian Influenza (AI) is a highly contagious viral disease of birds caused by influenza A viruses. It is classified by pathogenicity into two forms: **Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza (HPAI)** and **Low Pathogenic Avian Influenza (LPAI)**. HPAI — particularly strains H5N1, H5N8, and H7N9 — causes sudden, massive mortality in poultry and is a notifiable disease to WOAH and national veterinary authorities. Some HPAI strains can infect humans, making Avian Influenza a disease of major public health concern. Any suspected HPAI outbreak must be reported to authorities immediately.

### Causes

Avian Influenza is caused by influenza A viruses classified by two surface proteins:

- **Haemagglutinin (H)** — 18 subtypes (H1–H18)
- **Neuraminidase (N)** — 11 subtypes (N1–N11)

Pathogenicity classification:

- **HPAI** — caused by H5 and H7 subtypes with specific genetic features; mortality in poultry can reach 100% within 48 hours.
- **LPAI** — caused by all other subtypes; mild respiratory signs; low mortality in poultry.

HPAI H5N1 and H5N8 are the strains most frequently detected in Africa and pose the greatest risk to poultry and human health.

## Symptoms

Symptoms differ significantly between HPAI and LPAI.

### HPAI Signs (Severe Form)

- Sudden death with no prior signs — birds found dead in the morning
- Severe depression, weakness, and inability to stand
- Swollen head, face, and wattles (oedema)
- Purple-blue discolouration of comb, wattles, and legs (cyanosis)
- Haemorrhages on the legs and feet
- Severe respiratory distress — gasping and rattling
- Neurological signs — twisted neck, tremors, loss of coordination
- Sudden and complete cessation of egg production
- Watery green diarrhoea
- Mortality can reach 100% within 24–48 hours in unvaccinated flocks

### LPAI Signs (Mild Form)

- Mild respiratory signs — coughing, sneezing, nasal discharge
- Reduced feed and water intake
- Drop in egg production
- Soft-shelled or misshapen eggs
- Low mortality (usually less than 5%)

### Public Health Warning

Some HPAI strains — particularly H5N1 — can infect humans who have close contact with infected birds or their droppings. Human infection causes severe respiratory illness and can be fatal. Wear protective clothing when handling sick or dead birds during a suspected HPAI outbreak.

## Key Information

Avian Influenza is a notifiable disease. You are legally required to report suspected cases to your national veterinary authority. Do not attempt to manage an HPAI outbreak without official veterinary support.

### Transmission

Avian Influenza spreads through:

- Direct contact between infected and healthy birds
- Contaminated droppings — the primary route; virus is shed in large quantities
- Contaminated water sources, especially shared ponds and streams
- Wild waterfowl — ducks and geese are natural reservoirs and often show no signs
- Movement of live birds, eggs, and poultry products
- Contaminated equipment, vehicles, clothing, and footwear
- Airborne spread over short distances in enclosed housing

The virus survives in cool, moist environments for days to weeks. It is destroyed by heat (above 56 °C for 3 hours), sunlight, and standard disinfectants.

### Risk Factors

- Proximity to wild waterfowl habitats (lakes, rivers, wetlands)
- Open or free-range housing with access to wild birds
- Live bird markets in the area
- Unvaccinated flock in regions where vaccination is practised
- Poor biosecurity — shared equipment, unrestricted visitor access
- Introduction of birds from unknown sources
- Previous Avian Influenza outbreak in the region

### Diagnosis

**Do not attempt to diagnose Avian Influenza without veterinary support.** HPAI is a notifiable disease requiring official laboratory confirmation.

Field indicators that suggest HPAI:

- Sudden death of multiple birds with no prior signs
- Purple-blue discolouration of comb and wattles
- Swollen head and face
- Rapid spread through the flock within 24–48 hours

Report any suspected HPAI outbreak to your veterinarian or national veterinary authority immediately. Do not wait for laboratory confirmation before reporting.

## Treatment and Management

There is **no treatment** for Avian Influenza in poultry. HPAI outbreaks are managed through stamping out (culling) under government authority.

### Government Response to HPAI

When HPAI is confirmed, the national veterinary authority will:

1. Order the culling of all birds on the affected farm.
2. Establish a quarantine zone around the farm.
3. Trace and test all farms that received birds from the affected farm.
4. Supervise safe disposal of all carcasses, eggs, feed, and litter.
5. Disinfect the farm before restocking is permitted.

Cooperate fully with veterinary authorities. Attempting to hide an outbreak or sell birds from an infected farm is illegal and endangers public health.

### Personal Protection During Suspected HPAI

If you must handle sick or dead birds before veterinary authorities arrive:

- Wear gloves, a face mask, and protective clothing.
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after handling birds.
- Do not eat, drink, or smoke while handling birds.
- Seek medical attention immediately if you develop fever, cough, or difficulty breathing within 10 days of exposure.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Prevention combines biosecurity, surveillance, and vaccination where authorised.

### Biosecurity Measures

Biosecurity is the most important preventive measure against Avian Influenza:

- Prevent contact between your flock and wild birds — use netting or enclosed housing.
- Do not allow birds to access open water sources shared with wild waterfowl.
- Restrict visitor access to the farm.
- Disinfect vehicles, equipment, and footwear before entering the farm.
- Source birds only from disease-free, certified suppliers.
- Quarantine new birds for 14 days before introducing them to the flock.

### Vaccination

Vaccination against Avian Influenza is authorised in some African countries as part of national control programmes. Vaccination does not eliminate the virus but reduces mortality and virus shedding.

- Vaccination is only permitted where authorised by the national veterinary authority.
- Use only government-approved vaccines matched to the circulating strain.
- Vaccinated birds must still be monitored for signs of infection.
- Do not vaccinate without veterinary guidance — incorrect vaccination can mask infection and complicate surveillance.

### Surveillance

Report any unusual mortality or sudden drop in egg production to your veterinarian or livestock officer. Early reporting enables rapid response and limits spread.

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to protect your flock from Avian Influenza:

1. Prevent contact between your flock and wild birds at all times.
2. Do not allow birds to access open water shared with wild waterfowl.
3. Restrict and record all visitors to the farm.
4. Disinfect footwear and equipment before entering the poultry house.
5. Source birds only from reputable, disease-free suppliers.
6. Report any sudden unexplained mortality to your veterinarian immediately.
7. Cooperate fully with veterinary authorities if an outbreak is suspected.

## When to Call a Veterinarian

**Call a veterinarian or your national veterinary authority immediately** if you observe:

- Sudden death of multiple birds with no prior signs
- Purple-blue discolouration of comb, wattles, or legs
- Swollen head or face in multiple birds
- More than 10% of the flock dead within 24 hours
- Any combination of sudden death, neurological signs, and respiratory distress

**Do not wait.** HPAI spreads extremely fast. Delayed reporting increases the risk of spread to neighbouring farms and to humans.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If you suspect an HPAI outbreak:

1. Stop all movement of birds, people, and equipment on and off the farm immediately.
2. Call your veterinarian or national veterinary authority — do not wait.
3. Do not sell, move, or slaughter any birds.
4. Wear gloves and a face mask if you must handle sick or dead birds.
5. Do not eat birds that have died from suspected Avian Influenza.
6. Wash hands thoroughly after any contact with birds.

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist if Avian Influenza is suspected:

- [ ] All bird movement stopped immediately
- [ ] Veterinarian or national veterinary authority contacted
- [ ] No birds sold, moved, or slaughtered
- [ ] Protective clothing worn when handling sick or dead birds
- [ ] Dead birds not eaten or sold
- [ ] Hands washed after bird contact
- [ ] Farm access restricted to essential personnel only
- [ ] Neighbouring farmers alerted
