# Coccidiosis

## Overview

Coccidiosis is a common and economically important intestinal disease of poultry caused by microscopic parasites of the genus *Eimeria*. It affects chickens of all ages but is most severe in young chicks between three and six weeks of age. Coccidiosis causes bloody or watery diarrhoea, poor growth, and death in severe cases. The disease spreads through contaminated litter and droppings and is present on almost every poultry farm worldwide.

### Causes

Coccidiosis is caused by several species of *Eimeria*, each targeting a different part of the intestine. The most important species in chickens are:

- *Eimeria tenella* — caecal coccidiosis; causes bloody droppings; most lethal species.
- *Eimeria necatrix* — small intestine; severe haemorrhage; high mortality.
- *Eimeria acervulina* — upper small intestine; reduced absorption; poor growth.
- *Eimeria maxima* — mid small intestine; orange mucus in droppings.
- *Eimeria brunetti* — lower small intestine and rectum; watery diarrhoea.

A single bird can be infected with multiple *Eimeria* species at the same time.

## Symptoms

Symptoms vary by *Eimeria* species and the severity of infection.

### Mild to Moderate Infection

- Watery or loose droppings
- Reduced feed and water intake
- Ruffled feathers and hunched posture
- Pale comb and wattles
- Slow growth and poor feed conversion

### Severe Infection (Caecal Coccidiosis)

- Bright red or dark brown bloody droppings — the most recognisable sign
- Severe depression and weakness
- Birds huddling together under heat sources
- Rapid weight loss
- High mortality in chicks aged three to six weeks

### Post-infection Effects

Birds that survive a severe infection may have permanent intestinal damage. This leads to poor growth, reduced egg production in layers, and increased susceptibility to other diseases.

## Key Information

Coccidiosis is caused by oocysts — microscopic eggs shed in droppings. Understanding the lifecycle helps farmers break the cycle of infection.

### Transmission

Coccidiosis spreads through the faecal-oral route:

1. Infected birds shed oocysts in their droppings.
2. Oocysts sporulate (become infective) in warm, moist litter within 24–48 hours.
3. Healthy birds ingest sporulated oocysts from contaminated litter, feed, or water.
4. Oocysts multiply inside the intestinal wall, causing damage and more oocyst shedding.

Oocysts are highly resistant to environmental conditions. They survive in litter for months and are not killed by most common disinfectants. Heat, desiccation, and ammonia-based disinfectants reduce oocyst survival.

### Risk Factors

The following conditions increase the risk of coccidiosis outbreaks:

- Wet, damp litter — the most important risk factor
- High stocking density
- Poor ventilation causing moisture build-up
- Chicks aged three to six weeks (peak susceptibility)
- Failure to use coccidiostats in feed or water
- Mixing birds of different ages
- Contaminated equipment moved between pens

### Diagnosis

Coccidiosis is diagnosed by clinical signs and laboratory examination of droppings or intestinal contents.

Field indicators:

- Bloody droppings in chicks aged three to six weeks
- Sudden increase in mortality in young birds
- Wet litter combined with poor growth

Laboratory confirmation requires microscopic examination of droppings (oocyst count) or post-mortem examination of the intestines. A veterinarian should confirm the diagnosis and identify the *Eimeria* species involved.

## Treatment and Management

Coccidiosis can be treated with anticoccidial drugs. Treatment is most effective when started early, at the first sign of bloody droppings or increased mortality.

### Anticoccidial Treatment

The following drug classes are used to treat coccidiosis. Use only on veterinary advice and follow withdrawal periods before slaughter.

- **Amprolium** — inhibits thiamine uptake in *Eimeria*; administered in drinking water.
- **Sulfonamides** (e.g., sulfadimidine, sulfaquinoxaline) — broad-spectrum; administered in water or feed.
- **Toltrazuril** — highly effective against all *Eimeria* species; administered in water.
- **Diclazuril** — effective at low doses; administered in water.

Do not use anticoccidial drugs in birds that have received live coccidiosis vaccines, as the drugs will kill the vaccine organisms.

### Supportive Care

1. Improve litter management immediately — remove wet litter and replace with dry material.
2. Provide clean, fresh water at all times.
3. Add vitamin K and vitamin A supplements to drinking water to support intestinal recovery.
4. Reduce stocking density if overcrowding is a contributing factor.
5. Increase house temperature slightly for chicks showing severe depression.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Prevention combines coccidiostats in feed, good litter management, and vaccination.

### Coccidiostats in Feed

Coccidiostats are drugs added to feed at low concentrations to prevent coccidiosis rather than treat it. Common coccidiostats include:

- Ionophores: monensin, salinomycin, narasin, lasalocid
- Chemical coccidiostats: amprolium, diclazuril, robenidine

Rotate coccidiostat classes between flocks to prevent resistance. Do not use ionophore coccidiostats with tiamulin antibiotic — this combination is toxic to birds.

### Vaccination

Live attenuated coccidiosis vaccines are available and are used in broiler and layer replacement flocks. Vaccines are administered in the hatchery or at day one via spray or in feed. Vaccinated birds develop immunity through controlled exposure to low doses of *Eimeria* oocysts.

Vaccination is an alternative to coccidiostats, not a supplement. Do not use anticoccidial drugs in vaccinated birds.

### Litter Management

- Maintain litter moisture below 30% — the single most effective prevention measure.
- Improve ventilation to reduce condensation.
- Remove and replace wet patches of litter promptly.
- Clean and disinfect the house between flocks using ammonia-based or heat-based methods.

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to prevent and control coccidiosis:

1. Use coccidiostats in starter and grower feed for unvaccinated broilers.
2. Monitor litter moisture daily — wet litter is the primary risk factor.
3. Check droppings every morning for blood or unusual colour.
4. Weigh a sample of birds weekly to detect poor growth early.
5. Start treatment immediately if bloody droppings appear — do not wait.
6. Consult a veterinarian to confirm the diagnosis and select the correct drug.
7. Complete the full treatment course even if birds appear to recover.
8. Clean and disinfect the house thoroughly between flocks.

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Call a veterinarian immediately if you observe:

- Bloody droppings in more than five birds
- More than 3% of chicks dead within 24 hours
- Rapid spread of depression and huddling through the flock
- No improvement within 48 hours of starting treatment
- Recurrent outbreaks despite using coccidiostats

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If you see bloody droppings and birds dying rapidly:

1. Separate severely affected birds from the rest of the flock.
2. Start anticoccidial treatment in drinking water immediately — on veterinary advice.
3. Replace wet litter with dry material.
4. Add vitamin K to drinking water to reduce intestinal bleeding.
5. Call your veterinarian or livestock officer.

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist to monitor and prevent coccidiosis:

- [ ] Coccidiostat included in starter and grower feed (unvaccinated flocks)
- [ ] Litter checked daily for moisture and bloody droppings
- [ ] Ventilation adequate to keep litter dry
- [ ] Birds weighed weekly to detect poor growth
- [ ] Anticoccidial drugs available on farm for emergency use
- [ ] Veterinarian contact number available
- [ ] House cleaned and disinfected between flocks
