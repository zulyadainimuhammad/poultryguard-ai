# Fowl Pox

## Overview

Fowl pox is a viral disease of chickens and turkeys caused by an Avipoxvirus. The disease is characterized by lesions on unfeathered areas (skin pox — the "dry" form) or in the mouth and throat (diphtheric form — the "wet" form), or both simultaneously. The disease spreads slowly and has low mortality but causes poor growth, reduced egg production, and condemnation of carcasses at processing. Fowl pox is significant in tropical regions where biting insects (mosquitoes, midges) transmit the virus.

### Causes

Fowl pox is caused by *Fowlpox virus* (FPXV), a genus Avipoxvirus in the family Poxviridae. The virus is large and can be seen under light microscopy, unlike most other viruses. Multiple strains exist, and immunity to one strain provides partial protection against others. The virus is highly resistant to environmental conditions and can persist in the poultry house for years.

## Symptoms

Fowl pox typically appears 10–14 days after infection. The disease progresses slowly, and birds may remain infected for 3–6 weeks.

### Cutaneous Form (Dry Pox)

- Small, raised papules (bumps) on unfeathered areas: wattle, comb, face, eyes, and legs
- Lesions are initially yellowish and progress to brown or black scabs
- Lesions cluster together, covering the face and head
- Severe facial swelling can impair vision
- Pruritus (itching) — birds scratch lesions, causing bleeding
- Lesions last 2–4 weeks; scabs fall off, leaving temporary bald patches
- Mortality is low (< 5%)

### Diphtheritic Form (Wet Pox)

- Diphtheritic plaques (false membranes) in the mouth, pharynx, and larynx
- Difficulty swallowing and respiratory distress
- Drooling and anorexia
- Yellow or white plaques may be loosely attached
- Gasping and coughing
- Can lead to airway obstruction and suffocation
- Mortality higher than cutaneous form (up to 10–15%)

### Mixed Form

- Both skin lesions and respiratory signs simultaneously
- Worse prognosis than either form alone
- Respiratory compromise combined with reduced feed intake
- Highest mortality (up to 20%)

### Mortality Pattern

Mortality from fowl pox ranges from 0–20% depending on virus strain, bird age, and presence of secondary infections. Morbidity is high — 80–100% of susceptible flocks become infected. Deaths are often caused by starvation (diphtheritic form), airway obstruction, or secondary bacterial infections.

## Key Information

Fowl pox spreads slowly compared to other viral diseases. The virus is transmitted by biting insects and direct contact. It is highly resistant to environmental degradation.

### Transmission

Fowl pox spreads by:

- Biting insects (mosquitoes, gnats, midges) — primary vector, especially in tropical regions
- Direct contact between infected and susceptible birds
- Contaminated equipment, clothing, and footwear
- Contaminated feed and water (less efficient)
- Movement of infected birds and their products
- Respiratory secretions (diphtheritic form)

The virus survives in the environment for long periods:
- Several weeks on contaminated equipment
- Months in dried scabs
- Years in poultry house dust

The virus is inactivated by:
- High temperature (70 °C for 1 hour)
- Sunlight and UV radiation (slowly)
- Standard disinfectants (bleach, iodine, phenol) at recommended concentrations

### Risk Factors

- Presence of biting insects (mosquitoes, gnats, sandflies)
- Outdoor housing or poor insect control
- Introduction of infected birds
- Unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated flock
- Poor vaccination technique or administration
- Age — younger birds more susceptible
- Concurrent infections (Newcastle, Infectious Bursal Disease)
- Poor nutrition and stress

### Diagnosis

Fowl pox is diagnosed by characteristic skin lesions and respiratory signs, combined with laboratory confirmation.

Field indicators:

- Characteristic scabby lesions on unfeathered skin
- Lesions clustered on face, wattles, and comb
- Slow spread through the flock (days to weeks)
- Respiratory signs (diphtheritic form)
- Combination of skin and respiratory signs

Laboratory confirmation:
- Histopathology of biopsy material
- PCR testing
- Electron microscopy of lesion material (virus visible)
- Viral culture in cell lines or embryos

## Treatment and Management

There is **no specific antiviral treatment** for fowl pox. Management focuses on supportive care, secondary infection prevention, and insect control.

### Supportive Care

1. Ensure adequate feed and water — birds with oral lesions may struggle to eat
2. Provide softened or wet feed to facilitate intake
3. Treat secondary bacterial infections on veterinary advice
4. Improve ventilation if respiratory form is present
5. Control biting insects — use insect screens and repellents
6. Minimize stress and prevent mixing of infected and healthy birds if possible
7. Isolate severely affected birds if housing permits
8. Remove dead birds and treat lesions with disinfectant spray

### Carcass Management

- Birds that die from fowl pox or have extensive lesions must be condemned at processing
- Dispose of dead birds safely — burial or incineration
- Do not eat poultry from infected flocks until completely recovered

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Vaccination is the most effective prevention. Insect control reduces virus transmission.

### Vaccination (Recommended)

Fowl pox vaccines are live attenuated (weakened) virus vaccines, typically administered by wing web stick or feather follicle (scarification) method. Vaccination creates a local reaction at the injection site (small swelling and scab) — this is normal and indicates a successful vaccination.

Vaccination schedule:

- **Day-old chicks**: May be vaccinated in the hatchery
- **8–10 weeks of age**: Optimal vaccination time if not done at hatchery
- **Broilers**: Vaccinate if production cycle is long (> 10 weeks) or if fowl pox is endemic
- **Layers**: Vaccinate before 16 weeks of age; booster at 12–16 weeks if needed

Vaccinated birds develop local scabs and temporary immunity lasting 1–2 years. Revaccination is recommended if exposure risk remains high. Vaccination does not prevent infection but reduces severity and mortality.

### Insect Control

- Use insect screens on windows and ventilation openings
- Install insect-proof netting in outdoor runs
- Apply insect repellents (pyrethrins) to housing if needed
- Control standing water (mosquito breeding sites)
- Remove vegetation near housing that attracts biting insects
- House birds inside during peak mosquito hours (dawn and dusk)

### Biosecurity Measures

- Quarantine new birds for 14 days before introducing them to the flock
- Ensure birds are vaccinated before arrival
- Do not mix vaccinated and unvaccinated flocks
- Disinfect equipment and clothing between flocks
- Maintain strict visitor restrictions
- Prevent contact with wild birds
- Source birds only from reputable, vaccinated suppliers

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to prevent and manage fowl pox:

1. Vaccinate all birds at 8–10 weeks of age — this is the most effective prevention
2. Verify vaccination status of new birds before introduction
3. Implement insect control — screens, repellents, water management
4. Monitor for skin lesions and respiratory signs weekly
5. Isolate birds with severe respiratory signs if possible
6. Provide softened feed for birds with oral lesions
7. Treat secondary bacterial infections on veterinary advice
8. Do not comingle infected and uninfected flocks
9. Clean and disinfect housing between flocks
10. Dispose of dead birds safely

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Call a veterinarian if you observe:

- Respiratory distress combined with lesions (diphtheritic form)
- Severe facial swelling causing eye closure
- Stridor (breathing sounds) or airway obstruction signs
- Secondary signs (high fever, severe depression) suggesting secondary infection
- Unexplained mortality in conjunction with skin lesions
- Vaccination reactions that do not resolve within 2 weeks

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If you suspect fowl pox with respiratory involvement:

1. Isolate severely affected birds
2. Improve ventilation immediately
3. Call your veterinarian or livestock officer
4. Implement insect control measures
5. Disinfect all equipment and housing
6. Remove dead birds
7. Treat secondary infections on veterinary advice

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist for fowl pox prevention and management:

- [ ] All birds vaccinated at 8–10 weeks
- [ ] Vaccination records kept and verified
- [ ] Insect screens installed on ventilation openings
- [ ] Standing water removed and drainage improved
- [ ] Weekly monitoring for skin lesions or respiratory signs
- [ ] Insect repellents available if needed
- [ ] Biosecurity maintained (no outside contacts)
- [ ] Secondary infection treatments available
- [ ] Veterinarian contact number available
- [ ] Dead birds disposed of safely
