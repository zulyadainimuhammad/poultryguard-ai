# Fowl Pox

## Overview

Fowl pox is a viral disease of chickens, turkeys, and other poultry caused by an avipoxvirus. The disease causes wart-like skin lesions (scabs) on the head, legs, and wings, and respiratory symptoms in more severe cases. Fowl pox is present throughout Africa and is particularly common in free-ranging flocks and birds with poor management. The disease spreads slowly through flocks but causes chronic morbidity (illness) and reduced production. Mortality is typically low (< 5%), but economic losses from slow growth and reduced egg production are significant.

### Causes

Fowl pox is caused by avipoxviruses (distinct from mammalian poxviruses). The disease spreads through:

- **Respiratory droplets** — sneezing and coughing from infected birds
- **Direct contact** — contact with skin lesions from infected birds
- **Contaminated surfaces** — virus survives on skin scabs for extended periods
- **Mechanical transmission** — mosquitoes and other biting insects can transmit virus
- **Contaminated equipment** — feeders, drinkers, perches
- **Environment** — virus persists in poultry houses and pastures

The virus replicates in skin cells, forming characteristic wart-like lesions (scabs). Older birds and birds in poor condition more severely affected than young, healthy birds.

## Symptoms

Symptoms appear 4–11 days after infection. Two forms exist: dry form (skin lesions) and wet form (respiratory symptoms). Many birds show only mild signs.

### Dry Form (Skin Lesions) — Most Common

- **Wart-like scabs** — on face, comb, wattles, eyelids
- **Lesion progression** — small nodules → crusty scabs → sloughing off
- **Timing** — lesions appear 4–11 days post-infection; persist 2–4 weeks
- **Appearance** — rough, brown to black scabs, sometimes raised
- **Location** — head, neck, legs, wings; minimal on feathered areas
- **Secondary infection** — scabs may become infected with bacteria
- **Cosmetic damage** — reduces market value of affected birds
- **Minimal systemic signs** — birds remain relatively active and eating

### Wet Form (Respiratory Symptoms) — Less Common, More Severe

- **Diphtherial membranes** — yellowish, cheesy deposits in mouth and throat
- **Respiratory distress** — gasping, difficulty breathing
- **Nasal discharge** — watery; may be blood-tinged
- **Inability to swallow** — food may come out of nostrils
- **Severe depression** — bird sits quietly; ruffled feathers
- **Reduced feeding** — due to oral lesions
- **Higher mortality** — 5–50% compared to dry form
- **Sudden death** — if airway blocked by diphtheritic membranes

### Disease Course

- **Morbidity** — 5–50% of flock affected (spread is slow)
- **Mortality** — 1–10% in dry form; 10–50% in wet form
- **Duration** — acute phase 2–4 weeks; chronic signs persist longer
- **Recovery** — most birds recover; scabs slough off and feathers regrow
- **Immunity** — recovered birds immune to reinfection (strain-specific)

## Diagnosis

### On-Farm Diagnosis

Based on clinical signs:

- Wart-like scabs on face, comb, wattles
- Scabs persistent for weeks
- Slow spread through flock
- Minimal systemic signs (birds eating, active)
- History of poor management or outdoor ranging

### Veterinary Diagnosis

A veterinarian confirms diagnosis by:

- **Clinical signs** — characteristic warts and scabs highly suggestive
- **Histopathology** — skin biopsy showing characteristic viral inclusions (eosinophilic bodies)
- **PCR** — detects viral DNA
- **Electron microscopy** — demonstrates characteristic poxvirus morphology

## Prevention and Vaccination

### Biosecurity Measures

1. **Vaccination** — live attenuated vaccine available; administered by scarification (wing web)
2. **Isolation** — separate infected birds from uninfected flocks
3. **Reduce biting insects** — remove standing water; use insecticides if needed
4. **Avoid mechanical transmission** — disinfect equipment between flocks
5. **Quarantine new birds** — 2–3 weeks before mixing
6. **Reduce outdoor ranging** — minimize exposure to wild birds and insects
7. **Good sanitation** — clean and disinfect housing regularly
8. **Stress reduction** — minimize crowding and environmental stress

### Vaccination

Live attenuated fowl pox vaccine is available:

- **Route** — wing web scarification (needle scratch; vaccine introduced into skin)
- **Timing** — 8–12 weeks of age; before sexual maturity
- **Efficacy** — 90–95% protection against clinical disease
- **Duration** — lifetime immunity after single dose
- **Contraindications** — do not vaccinate if birds already infected
- **Vaccination take** — mild scab at vaccination site (normal; resolves in 2–3 weeks)

## Treatment and Management

**No specific antiviral treatment exists.** Management is supportive:

### Supportive Care

1. **Isolation** — move sick birds to separate, well-ventilated pen (optional)
2. **Water and feed** — ensure access; soft feed if mouth/throat lesions present
3. **Wound care** — if secondary bacterial infection suspected, topical antiseptic may help
4. **Insect control** — minimize mosquito and insect exposure
5. **Monitoring** — watch for secondary infections (bacterial, fungal)
6. **Stress reduction** — minimize handling; maintain comfortable environment

### Antibiotic for Secondary Infection

If secondary bacterial infection suspected (increased mortality, fever):

- Consult veterinarian for antimicrobial choice
- Common: tetracycline, amoxicillin, or enrofloxacin
- Duration: 5–7 days

### Recovery

- Most birds recover spontaneously
- Scabs slough off within 2–4 weeks
- Feathers regrow; cosmetic damage resolves
- Recovered birds immune to reinfection

### Management

### During Outbreak

1. **Identify affected birds** — isolate to separate pen (reduce transmission)
2. **Monitor all birds** — check daily for new cases
3. **Maintain biosecurity** — do not share equipment; wash hands between birds
4. **Reduce insect exposure** — eliminate standing water; use insecticides if needed
5. **Good sanitation** — clean and disinfect housing
6. **Avoid stress** — minimize handling; ensure adequate feed/water
7. **Treat secondary infections** — consult veterinarian if needed

### Post-Outbreak

1. **Plan vaccination** — vaccinate entire flock at appropriate age if not already done
2. **Clean and disinfect** — spray housing with disinfectant
3. **Reduce insect exposure** — continue preventive measures
4. **Monitor for recurrence** — watch for new cases (immunity should prevent)

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Contact a veterinarian if you observe:

- Wart-like scabs on multiple birds
- Unsure if lesions are fowl pox or another condition
- Respiratory signs (gasping, difficulty breathing) in addition to skin lesions
- Signs of secondary bacterial infection (fever, increased mortality, high fever)
- Mortality increasing beyond expected 1–5%
- Interest in vaccination program for flock protection

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If wet form (respiratory) disease occurs:

1. Isolate affected birds immediately
2. Provide easy access to water (essential if swallowing difficult)
3. Contact veterinarian urgently; symptoms may require antimicrobial treatment
4. Remove any diphtheritic membranes blocking airway (if safe to do so)
5. Improve ventilation; reduce dust and ammonia
6. Monitor for secondary bacterial infection

## Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist for fowl pox management:

- [ ] Wart-like scabs recognized on birds' heads/faces
- [ ] Affected birds isolated to separate pen (optional)
- [ ] Veterinarian contacted for diagnosis confirmation
- [ ] All birds monitored daily for new lesions
- [ ] Feeders and drinkers checked for contamination; cleaned if needed
- [ ] Soft feed available if mouth/throat lesions present
- [ ] Biting insects managed: standing water eliminated; insecticides used if needed
- [ ] Buildings cleaned and disinfected
- [ ] Secondary bacterial infection assessed; antimicrobial prescribed if needed
- [ ] Vaccination program planned (birds 8–12 weeks old)
- [ ] Vaccination performed by trained person (wing web scarification)
- [ ] Vaccination take monitored (small scab at vaccination site expected)
- [ ] Entire flock vaccinated for future protection
- [ ] Recovered birds retained (immune to reinfection)
- [ ] No birds sold or traded during active outbreak
