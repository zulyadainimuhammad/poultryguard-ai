# Gumboro Disease

## Overview

Gumboro disease (Infectious Bursal Disease, IBD) is a highly contagious viral disease of young chickens caused by *Infectious bursal disease virus* (IBDV), a birnavirus. It primarily affects chicks aged three to six weeks by destroying the bursa of Fabricius — the organ responsible for developing the immune system. Gumboro disease causes direct mortality and, more importantly, permanent immunosuppression that leaves surviving birds vulnerable to other infections. It is a major cause of economic loss in African poultry production.

### Causes

Gumboro disease is caused by IBDV, which exists in two serotypes:

- **Serotype 1** — pathogenic to chickens; causes clinical disease.
- **Serotype 2** — infects chickens and turkeys; not pathogenic.

Within serotype 1, two variants are important:

- **Classic strains** — cause typical Gumboro disease with moderate mortality.
- **Very virulent strains (vvIBDV)** — cause mortality up to 60–70% in unvaccinated flocks; widespread in Africa.

IBDV is extremely resistant in the environment. It survives in contaminated houses for months and is resistant to many disinfectants.

## Symptoms

Gumboro disease has two phases: an acute clinical phase and a subclinical immunosuppressive phase.

### Acute Phase (Clinical Disease)

The acute phase occurs in chicks aged three to six weeks:

- Sudden onset of depression and weakness
- Ruffled feathers and huddling
- Watery, whitish diarrhoea
- Soiled vent feathers
- Trembling and unsteady gait
- Pecking at their own vent (due to irritation)
- Rapid dehydration
- Mortality typically peaks at days three to five after onset

Mortality in unvaccinated flocks infected with very virulent strains can reach 60–70%.

### Subclinical Phase (Immunosuppression)

Chicks infected before three weeks of age often show no obvious clinical signs but suffer severe immunosuppression:

- Bursa of Fabricius is destroyed or severely damaged
- Surviving birds cannot mount effective immune responses
- Vaccines given after infection may fail to protect
- Birds become highly susceptible to Newcastle disease, Marek's disease, and bacterial infections
- Poor growth and increased feed conversion ratio

Immunosuppression is the most economically damaging consequence of Gumboro disease.

## Key Information

Gumboro disease is difficult to eradicate once it enters a farm because the virus persists in the environment for months.

### Transmission

IBDV spreads through:

- Direct contact between infected and healthy birds
- Contaminated droppings — the primary route
- Contaminated litter, feed, water, and equipment
- Contaminated clothing and footwear of farm workers
- Insects (darkling beetles) acting as mechanical vectors
- Contaminated hatcheries spreading virus to day-old chicks

IBDV does not spread through eggs (no vertical transmission). However, contaminated hatchery equipment can infect chicks before they reach the farm.

### Risk Factors

- Chicks aged three to six weeks — peak susceptibility
- Unvaccinated or inadequately vaccinated flock
- Contaminated house not properly cleaned between flocks
- Introduction of birds from infected sources
- Presence of darkling beetles in litter
- Previous Gumboro disease outbreak on the farm

### Diagnosis

Gumboro disease is diagnosed by clinical signs, post-mortem findings, and laboratory tests.

Field indicators:

- Sudden depression and diarrhoea in chicks aged three to six weeks
- Rapid spread through the flock
- Birds pecking at their own vent
- High mortality peaking at days three to five

Post-mortem findings include an enlarged, haemorrhagic bursa of Fabricius in the acute phase, and a shrunken, atrophied bursa in the subclinical phase. A veterinarian should confirm the diagnosis.

## Treatment and Management

There is **no specific antiviral treatment** for Gumboro disease. Management focuses on supportive care and preventing secondary infections.

### Immediate Actions

1. Isolate affected birds from the rest of the flock.
2. Provide clean, fresh water with electrolytes and glucose to combat dehydration.
3. Increase house temperature by 2–3 °C to reduce cold stress in depressed birds.
4. Remove wet litter and replace with dry material.
5. Reduce stocking density if overcrowding is present.
6. Contact a veterinarian to confirm the diagnosis.

### Supportive Care

- Add electrolytes and vitamin C to drinking water to support recovery.
- Treat secondary bacterial infections (e.g., *Escherichia coli*) only on veterinary advice.
- Provide high-quality feed to support immune recovery in surviving birds.
- Monitor surviving birds closely for signs of secondary disease in the weeks following the outbreak.

### Carcass Disposal

Remove and safely dispose of dead birds immediately. Bury at least 1 metre deep or incinerate. Do not leave carcasses in the pen.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Vaccination is the primary method of preventing Gumboro disease. Because IBDV persists in the environment, vaccination must be combined with strict biosecurity.

### Vaccination

Both live attenuated and inactivated vaccines are available. The choice of vaccine depends on the level of maternal antibody in the chicks and the strain of IBDV present in the region.

| Vaccine Type | Use | Route |
|---|---|---|
| Live intermediate strain | Standard prevention in broilers | Drinking water or eye drop |
| Live intermediate-plus strain | Areas with very virulent strains | Drinking water |
| Inactivated oil-emulsion | Breeders and layers for maternal antibody transfer | Injection |

A typical broiler vaccination schedule:

| Age | Vaccine | Route |
|---|---|---|
| Day 14–18 | Live intermediate (e.g., D78, Lukert) | Drinking water |
| Day 24–28 | Live intermediate booster | Drinking water |

The exact timing depends on maternal antibody levels. Your veterinarian can advise on the correct timing using antibody testing (ELISA).

### Biosecurity Measures

- Clean and disinfect the house thoroughly between flocks using formaldehyde or glutaraldehyde-based disinfectants.
- Control darkling beetles — a major reservoir of IBDV on farms.
- Restrict visitor access and disinfect footwear.
- Source chicks only from Marek's disease and IBDV-vaccinated breeder flocks.

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to protect your flock from Gumboro disease:

1. Vaccinate all chicks on schedule — timing is critical.
2. Use the correct vaccine strain for your region — consult your veterinarian.
3. Ensure the vaccine cold chain is maintained from purchase to administration.
4. Control darkling beetles in litter — they carry IBDV between flocks.
5. Clean and disinfect the house completely between flocks.
6. Monitor chicks aged three to six weeks closely for depression and diarrhoea.
7. Report suspected outbreaks to your veterinarian immediately.

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Call a veterinarian immediately if you observe:

- More than 5% of chicks aged three to six weeks dead within 48 hours
- Sudden depression and diarrhoea spreading rapidly through the flock
- Birds pecking at their own vent
- No improvement within 48 hours of starting supportive care
- Recurring outbreaks despite vaccination

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If you suspect a Gumboro disease outbreak:

1. Isolate sick birds immediately.
2. Provide electrolytes and glucose in drinking water.
3. Increase house temperature by 2–3 °C.
4. Remove wet litter.
5. Call your veterinarian or livestock officer.
6. Do not introduce new birds to the farm.

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist during and after a suspected Gumboro disease outbreak:

- [ ] Sick birds isolated
- [ ] Electrolytes and glucose provided in water
- [ ] House temperature increased
- [ ] Wet litter removed and replaced
- [ ] Veterinarian contacted
- [ ] Dead birds buried or incinerated
- [ ] Vaccination records reviewed
- [ ] Darkling beetle control measures in place
