# Infectious Bronchitis

## Overview

Infectious Bronchitis (IB) is a highly contagious respiratory disease of chickens caused by *Infectious bronchitis virus* (IBV), a coronavirus. It affects the respiratory tract, kidneys, and reproductive system. In broilers, Infectious Bronchitis causes respiratory distress and poor growth. In layers, it causes a severe and often permanent drop in egg production and poor egg quality. Infectious Bronchitis is present worldwide and is one of the most economically important poultry diseases. Multiple IBV serotypes and variants exist, and immunity to one serotype does not protect against others.

### Causes

Infectious Bronchitis is caused by IBV, a member of the family *Coronaviridae*. IBV mutates rapidly, producing many serotypes and variants. Important serotypes include:

- **Massachusetts (Mass)** — the original and most widespread serotype; used as the basis for most vaccines.
- **4/91 (793B)** — common in Europe and Africa; causes respiratory and reproductive disease.
- **QX** — emerging variant; causes severe kidney damage (nephritis) and reproductive failure.
- **Other regional variants** — many local variants circulate in Africa; vaccine matching is important.

Because IBV mutates frequently, vaccines based on one serotype may not protect against field strains. Consult your veterinarian about the serotypes circulating in your region.

## Symptoms

Symptoms vary by age, serotype, and organ system affected.

### Respiratory Signs (All Ages)

- Gasping, coughing, sneezing, and rattling breathing sounds
- Nasal discharge and watery eyes
- Swollen sinuses
- Huddling under heat sources in chicks
- Reduced feed and water intake
- Mortality in young chicks can reach 25–30% if secondary bacterial infections occur

### Reproductive Signs (Layers and Breeders)

- Sudden and severe drop in egg production — often 50% or more
- Poor egg quality: thin shells, soft shells, misshapen eggs
- Watery albumen (egg white) — eggs appear watery when broken
- Reduced hatchability in breeders
- Permanent damage to the oviduct in chicks infected before sexual maturity — these birds never lay normally ("false layers")

### Kidney Signs (Nephropathogenic Strains)

Some IBV strains — particularly QX — cause kidney damage:

- Increased water intake and wet droppings
- Pale, swollen kidneys at post-mortem
- Urate deposits in kidneys and ureters (visceral gout)
- High mortality in young chicks (up to 30%)

## Key Information

Infectious Bronchitis spreads extremely rapidly. A single infected bird can infect an entire flock within 24–48 hours.

### Transmission

IBV spreads through:

- Airborne droplets — the primary and most rapid route
- Direct contact between infected and healthy birds
- Contaminated droppings, feed, water, and equipment
- Contaminated clothing and footwear
- Movement of infected birds

IBV survives in the environment for only a few days at room temperature but longer in cool conditions. It is destroyed by standard disinfectants and heat.

### Risk Factors

- Unvaccinated or inadequately vaccinated flock
- Chicks aged one to four weeks — most susceptible to severe respiratory disease
- Pullets infected before sexual maturity — risk of permanent oviduct damage
- Presence of multiple IBV serotypes in the region
- Poor ventilation — increases airborne virus concentration
- Concurrent infections with *Mycoplasma gallisepticum* or *E. coli* worsen respiratory disease
- Cold, damp weather — increases respiratory stress

### Diagnosis

Infectious Bronchitis is diagnosed by clinical signs and laboratory tests. Diagnosis is important because the serotype must be identified to select the correct vaccine.

Field indicators:

- Rapid spread of respiratory signs through the entire flock within 24–48 hours
- Sudden egg drop in layers with poor shell quality
- Watery albumen in broken eggs
- Respiratory signs in chicks with no response to antibiotic treatment

Laboratory confirmation requires virus isolation, PCR testing, or serology. A veterinarian should collect samples and arrange laboratory testing.

## Treatment and Management

There is **no specific antiviral treatment** for Infectious Bronchitis. Management focuses on supportive care and controlling secondary bacterial infections.

### Immediate Actions

1. Increase house temperature by 2–3 °C to reduce cold stress in affected chicks.
2. Improve ventilation to reduce ammonia and dust — but avoid cold draughts.
3. Provide clean, fresh water with electrolytes and vitamins.
4. Reduce stocking density if overcrowding is present.
5. Contact a veterinarian to confirm the diagnosis and identify the serotype.

### Controlling Secondary Infections

Secondary bacterial infections — particularly *E. coli* and *Mycoplasma* — significantly worsen Infectious Bronchitis. Treat secondary infections on veterinary advice using appropriate antibiotics. Do not use antibiotics without a veterinary prescription.

### Supportive Care

- Add vitamin C and electrolytes to drinking water to support recovery.
- Provide high-quality feed to support immune function.
- Monitor kidney function in flocks affected by nephropathogenic strains — increase water availability.
- Remove and safely dispose of dead birds daily.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Vaccination is the primary method of preventing Infectious Bronchitis. Vaccine selection must match the serotypes circulating in the region.

### Vaccination

Live attenuated and inactivated vaccines are available. A typical vaccination programme:

| Age | Vaccine Type | Route |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 (hatchery) | Live attenuated (Mass or 4/91) | Spray or eye drop |
| Day 14–21 | Live attenuated booster | Drinking water or spray |
| 16–18 weeks (layers) | Inactivated oil-emulsion | Injection |

Key points for vaccination:

- Use vaccines matched to the serotypes in your region — consult your veterinarian.
- Maintain the cold chain — store vaccines at 2–8 °C.
- Do not mix live IBV vaccines with other live vaccines unless advised by the manufacturer.
- Inactivated vaccines given to layers before the start of lay provide longer-lasting protection.

### Biosecurity Measures

- Restrict visitor access and disinfect footwear before entering the poultry house.
- Quarantine new birds for 14 days before introducing them to the flock.
- Clean and disinfect the house thoroughly between flocks.
- Improve ventilation to reduce airborne virus concentration.

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to protect your flock from Infectious Bronchitis:

1. Vaccinate all chicks at day one with a live IBV vaccine matched to local serotypes.
2. Give a booster vaccine at two to three weeks of age.
3. Vaccinate layers with an inactivated vaccine before the start of lay.
4. Maintain good ventilation — reduce ammonia and dust without creating cold draughts.
5. Treat secondary bacterial infections promptly on veterinary advice.
6. Monitor egg production and quality daily in layers — a sudden drop is an early warning sign.
7. Consult your veterinarian about the IBV serotypes circulating in your region.

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Call a veterinarian if you observe:

- Respiratory signs spreading through the entire flock within 24–48 hours
- Egg production dropping by more than 20% in one week
- Watery albumen in eggs from multiple birds
- Respiratory signs not improving after 48 hours of supportive care
- High mortality in young chicks despite supportive treatment
- Suspected kidney disease — increased water intake and wet droppings

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If Infectious Bronchitis spreads rapidly through your flock:

1. Increase house temperature immediately.
2. Provide electrolytes and vitamins in drinking water.
3. Improve ventilation without creating cold draughts.
4. Call your veterinarian to confirm the diagnosis and serotype.
5. Do not introduce new birds to the farm during the outbreak.

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist to monitor and prevent Infectious Bronchitis:

- [ ] Vaccination programme in place and up to date
- [ ] Vaccine cold chain maintained
- [ ] Ventilation adequate — no ammonia smell, no cold draughts
- [ ] Egg production and quality monitored daily in layers
- [ ] Veterinarian contact number available
- [ ] Secondary bacterial infections treated on veterinary advice
- [ ] House cleaned and disinfected between flocks
