# Newcastle Disease

## Overview

Newcastle disease is a highly contagious and often fatal viral infection of poultry caused by virulent strains of *Avian orthoavulavirus 1* (formerly *Paramyxovirus avium* serotype 1). It affects chickens, turkeys, ducks, and many wild bird species. Newcastle disease is one of the most economically damaging poultry diseases in Africa, capable of killing an entire unvaccinated flock within days. The disease is notifiable to national veterinary authorities and to WOAH.

### Causes

Newcastle disease is caused by virulent strains of Avian orthoavulavirus 1 (AOAV-1). Strains are classified by pathogenicity:

- **Lentogenic strains** — mild or no clinical signs; used in live vaccines.
- **Mesogenic strains** — moderate disease; respiratory and nervous signs.
- **Velogenic strains** — severe disease; high mortality; two subtypes:
  - *Viscerotropic velogenic* — haemorrhagic lesions in the gut.
  - *Neurotropic velogenic* — respiratory and neurological signs.

Velogenic strains cause the outbreaks that devastate village and commercial flocks in Africa.

## Symptoms

Newcastle disease produces three overlapping clinical syndromes depending on the strain and the flock's immune status.

### Respiratory Signs

- Gasping, coughing, and sneezing
- Rattling or gurgling breathing sounds
- Nasal discharge and watery eyes
- Swelling around the eyes and neck

### Neurological Signs

- Twisted neck (torticollis) — a hallmark sign
- Paralysis of wings and legs
- Circling, falling, and inability to stand
- Tremors and convulsions

### Digestive Signs

- Greenish, watery diarrhoea
- Loss of appetite and rapid weight loss
- Sudden drop in egg production
- Soft-shelled or misshapen eggs in layers

### Mortality Pattern

Mortality in unvaccinated flocks can reach 100% within five to seven days of the first signs. Vaccinated flocks may show milder signs with lower mortality.

## Key Information

Newcastle disease spreads rapidly and has no cure. Early recognition and immediate action are the only ways to limit losses.

### Transmission

Newcastle disease virus spreads through:

- Direct contact between sick and healthy birds
- Contaminated droppings, feathers, and secretions
- Contaminated feed, water, equipment, and clothing
- Movement of live birds, eggs, and carcasses
- Wild birds entering the farm
- Airborne spread over short distances

The virus survives in the environment for several weeks in cool, moist conditions. It is destroyed by heat, sunlight, and common disinfectants.

### Risk Factors

The following conditions increase the risk of an outbreak:

- Unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated flock
- Introduction of new birds without quarantine
- Shared equipment between farms
- Poor biosecurity — open housing, free-ranging birds near wild birds
- Live bird markets in the area
- Previous outbreak on the farm or in the region

### Diagnosis

Newcastle disease is diagnosed by a combination of clinical signs and laboratory tests. Diagnosis must be confirmed by a veterinarian or government laboratory.

Field indicators that suggest Newcastle disease:

- Sudden onset of respiratory signs in multiple birds
- Neurological signs (twisted neck, paralysis)
- Rapid spread through the flock
- High mortality in unvaccinated birds

Laboratory confirmation requires virus isolation or PCR testing. Do not attempt to diagnose Newcastle disease without veterinary support.

## Treatment and Management

There is **no specific antiviral treatment** for Newcastle disease. Management focuses on limiting spread and supporting surviving birds.

### Immediate Actions

1. Isolate all sick birds from the rest of the flock immediately.
2. Stop all movement of birds, equipment, and people between pens.
3. Report the suspected outbreak to your local veterinary authority.
4. Do not sell or move birds from the farm until a veterinarian advises it is safe.
5. Provide clean water and electrolytes to surviving birds to reduce stress.
6. Increase ventilation to reduce respiratory distress.

### Supportive Care

- Provide vitamin and electrolyte supplements in drinking water to support recovery.
- Treat secondary bacterial infections only on veterinary advice.
- Remove and safely dispose of dead birds — do not leave carcasses in the pen.

### Carcass Disposal

Bury dead birds at least 1 metre deep, away from water sources, or incinerate them. Do not sell or eat birds that died from suspected Newcastle disease.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent Newcastle disease. Biosecurity measures reduce the risk of virus introduction.

### Vaccination

Live attenuated vaccines using lentogenic strains (La Sota, B1, Clone 30, V4) are widely used in Africa. The following schedule is a general guide; consult your veterinarian for the schedule appropriate to your region and flock type.

| Age | Vaccine Type | Route |
|---|---|---|
| Day 7–10 | Live lentogenic (La Sota or B1) | Eye drop or intranasal |
| Day 21–28 | Live lentogenic (La Sota) booster | Eye drop or drinking water |
| Every 8–12 weeks | Live lentogenic booster | Drinking water |

Inactivated (killed) oil-emulsion vaccines provide longer-lasting immunity and are used in layers and breeders. Administer inactivated vaccines by injection on veterinary advice.

Vaccine cold chain is critical. Store vaccines at 2–8 °C and use within the time stated on the label after opening.

### Biosecurity Measures

- Quarantine all new birds for 14 days before introducing them to the flock.
- Restrict visitor access to the farm.
- Disinfect footwear and equipment before entering the poultry house.
- Prevent contact between your flock and wild birds.
- Source birds and eggs only from reputable, disease-free suppliers.

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to protect your flock from Newcastle disease:

1. Vaccinate all birds on schedule — do not skip booster doses.
2. Keep a written vaccination record for every batch of birds.
3. Quarantine new birds for 14 days before mixing with your flock.
4. Disinfect the poultry house between batches.
5. Report any sudden increase in deaths or respiratory signs to a veterinarian immediately.
6. Do not buy birds from markets where Newcastle disease is known to be present.
7. Dispose of dead birds safely — bury or incinerate; never leave carcasses in the open.

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Call a veterinarian immediately if you observe any of the following:

- More than two birds showing twisted neck or paralysis
- More than 5% of the flock dead within 48 hours
- Sudden drop in egg production of more than 20% in one week
- Respiratory signs spreading rapidly through the flock
- Any combination of respiratory, neurological, and digestive signs

**Do not wait.** Newcastle disease spreads fast. Early veterinary intervention can save part of your flock and prevent spread to neighbouring farms.

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If you suspect a Newcastle disease outbreak:

1. Isolate sick birds immediately.
2. Stop all bird movement on and off the farm.
3. Call your veterinarian or local livestock officer.
4. Do not sell birds, eggs, or carcasses.
5. Disinfect all equipment and footwear.

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist during and after a suspected outbreak:

- [ ] Sick birds isolated from healthy birds
- [ ] Bird movement stopped
- [ ] Veterinarian or livestock officer contacted
- [ ] Dead birds buried or incinerated
- [ ] Equipment and footwear disinfected
- [ ] Vaccination records checked and updated
- [ ] Neighbouring farmers alerted
