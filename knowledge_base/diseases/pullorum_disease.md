# Pullorum Disease

## Overview

Pullorum disease is a bacterial infection of poultry caused by *Salmonella pullorum*. The disease primarily affects young chicks and is characterized by high mortality in birds less than 3 weeks of age. Older chickens may develop chronic infection with production losses and infertility. The disease is transmitted vertically (via contaminated eggs) and horizontally (between birds). In many developed countries, pullorum disease has been eradicated through strict hatchery testing and biosecurity, but it remains a significant problem in regions with poor hatchery controls.

### Causes

Pullorum disease is caused by *Salmonella enterica* subsp. *enterica* serovar *Pullorum* (S. pullorum). The bacterium survives in the reproductive tract of infected hens and can contaminate the inside of eggs before the shell hardens. Vertical transmission is the primary source of infection in young chicks. Horizontal transmission occurs through contaminated litter, feed, and water.

## Symptoms

Symptoms vary by age and route of infection.

### In Chicks (0–3 weeks)

- Lethargy and depression — chicks huddle and sleep excessively
- Loss of appetite and failure to thrive
- Dehydration — ruffled feathers and sunken eyes
- Pasty rear (dried faeces caking the vent area) — a hallmark sign
- Diarrhoea — may be yellow, white, or brown
- Breathing difficulty (respiratory involvement)
- Mortality peaks at 5–14 days of age

### In Older Birds (> 3 weeks)

- Persistent depression and poor growth
- Chronic diarrhoea
- Lameness or swelling of joints (arthritis)
- Pericarditis (inflammation around the heart) visible at post-mortem
- Eye inflammation and blindness (rare)
- Sudden death without prior signs (septicaemia)

### In Layers and Breeding Birds

- Reduced egg production (30–50% drop)
- Infertility in hens and cocks
- Soft-shelled or misshapen eggs
- Infected but asymptomatic hens shed bacteria in eggs
- Chronic systemic infection with minimal clinical signs

### Mortality Pattern

Mortality in infected chicks can reach 50–80% if untreated. Early treatment reduces mortality but may not prevent chronic infection. Older birds rarely die but suffer production losses and become persistent carriers.

## Key Information

Pullorum disease spreads primarily through infected eggs (vertical transmission) and secondarily through contact with infected birds. Early diagnosis and treatment are essential in young chicks.

### Transmission

Vertical transmission (via eggs):

1. *S. pullorum* survives in the ovary and intestines of infected hens
2. Bacteria contaminate eggs as they form in the ovary or as they pass through the oviduct
3. Infected eggs hatch infected chicks
4. Infected chicks are weak and susceptible; some die, others become chronic carriers

Horizontal transmission:

- Direct contact between infected and susceptible chicks
- Contaminated litter, feed, water
- Contaminated equipment and clothing
- Recovered birds that remain carriers and shed bacteria intermittently

The bacterium is killed by:
- Heat (70 °C for 1 hour)
- Sunlight and UV radiation
- Standard disinfectants (bleach, iodine, quaternary ammonia)

### Risk Factors

- Chicks from infected hatcheries (primary risk)
- Introduction of carrier birds
- Poor biosecurity — shared equipment, visitors
- High stocking density in hatcheries or brooder facilities
- Poor sanitation of hatchery equipment
- Contaminated bedding or feed
- Mixing birds of different ages
- Lack of hatchery testing programs

### Diagnosis

Pullorum disease is diagnosed by clinical signs in chicks, flock history, and laboratory confirmation.

Field indicators:

- Sudden high mortality in chicks < 3 weeks of age
- Pasty rear (dried faeces at the vent) — characteristic sign
- Depression and poor growth
- High morbidity with variable mortality
- History of vertical transmission (infected eggs from specific hens)

Laboratory confirmation:

- Bacterial culture from blood, organs (liver, spleen, heart)
- Biochemical tests to identify *Salmonella pullorum*
- Serology (blood test) — detects antibodies in older birds
- PCR testing for rapid identification

Hatchery testing:

- Hatchery flocks tested by serology (blood tests) to detect infected hens
- Eggs from positive hens condemned
- Strict hatchery sanitation and biosecurity protocols

## Treatment and Management

Pullorum disease responds well to antibiotics if treatment is started early. Treatment in chicks < 3 weeks old should begin immediately upon diagnosis.

### Antibiotic Treatment

Common antibiotics for pullorum disease:

- **Fluoroquinolones** (e.g., enrofloxacin) — highly effective; administered in drinking water
- **Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole** — administered in water
- **Gentamicin** — parenteral injection for severe cases
- **Tetracyclines** (e.g., doxycycline) — less effective; administered in water

Treatment duration is typically 5–10 days. However, treatment in chicks may not eliminate the infection — some birds may become chronic carriers despite antibiotics.

### Supportive Care

1. Start antibiotic treatment immediately upon diagnosis
2. Provide clean, fresh water with electrolytes
3. Offer softened or wet feed to encourage intake
4. Maintain chick temperature at 35 °C (95 °F) initially, then gradually reduce
5. Minimize stress — reduce handling and noise
6. Remove dead chicks immediately
7. Change litter if heavily contaminated
8. Isolate infected chicks if housing permits

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

## Prevention and Vaccination

Prevention focuses on hatchery testing, biosecurity, and sourcing chicks from disease-free hatcheries. No vaccine is available.

### Hatchery Testing (Critical)

- Breeder flocks must be tested regularly for *S. pullorum* antibodies
- Positive birds must be removed immediately
- Eggs from positive hens must be condemned
- Hatchery equipment must be cleaned and disinfected between batches
- Only purchase chicks from hatcheries with documented pullorum-free status

### Biosecurity Measures

- Source all chicks from reputable, tested hatcheries only
- Quarantine new birds for 2–3 weeks before introduction
- Do not mix chicks from different hatcheries during rearing
- Maintain strict visitor restrictions in hatchery and brooder areas
- Disinfect all equipment before use (waterers, feeders, brooders)
- Use separate clothing and footwear for hatchery areas
- Prevent wild bird and rodent access
- Dispose of dead chicks safely — burial or incineration

### Culling Protocol

If pullorum disease is detected in a flock:

1. Cull all infected chicks immediately to stop spread
2. Thoroughly disinfect brooder equipment
3. Replace infected birds with chicks from pullorum-free hatcheries
4. Do not retain breeder birds from infected flock
5. Test any replacement breeders before introducing to the flock

## Recommendations

Follow these steps to prevent pullorum disease:

1. **Purchase chicks ONLY from pullorum-tested, disease-free hatcheries** — this is the critical prevention measure
2. Verify hatchery testing status before ordering chicks
3. Implement strict biosecurity in brooder areas
4. Monitor chicks daily for signs of disease (pasty rear, depression)
5. Start antibiotic treatment immediately if clinical signs appear
6. Consult a veterinarian to confirm diagnosis and select antibiotics
7. Do not retain birds from infected flocks as breeders
8. Do not use infected birds' eggs for hatching
9. If maintaining a breeding flock, test for pullorum disease annually
10. Report suspected pullorum disease to local animal health authorities

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Call a veterinarian immediately if you observe:

- Chicks with pasty rear and depression
- Sudden high mortality in chicks < 3 weeks of age
- High morbidity combined with poor growth
- No improvement within 48 hours of starting treatment
- Chicks from a suspected infected hatchery

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If you suspect pullorum disease in chicks:

1. Start antibiotic treatment in drinking water immediately — on veterinary advice
2. Call your veterinarian or livestock officer
3. Disinfect all brooder equipment
4. Isolate chicks if possible or prepare to cull infected birds
5. Do not introduce these birds to older flocks
6. Record the hatchery source and report to authorities

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist for pullorum disease prevention:

- [ ] Chicks sourced from pullorum-tested hatchery only
- [ ] Hatchery testing status documented and filed
- [ ] All brooder equipment disinfected before chick arrival
- [ ] Daily monitoring for pasty rear or depression
- [ ] Antibiotic treatment available on farm
- [ ] Veterinarian contact number available
- [ ] Dead chicks removed immediately
- [ ] Separate footwear and clothing for brooder area
- [ ] No wild bird or rodent access
- [ ] If breeding birds retained, annual pullorum testing planned
