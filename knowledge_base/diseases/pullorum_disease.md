# Pullorum Disease

## Overview

Pullorum disease is a bacterial disease of poultry caused by *Salmonella pullorum*. The disease affects primarily young chicks through vertical transmission (infected eggs from carrier hens). Infected chicks develop severe diarrhoea, depression, and high mortality (50–90%). Pullorum disease is one of the most economically important diseases of hatcheries and chick-rearing operations. The disease has been eliminated from commercial poultry in many countries but remains present in parts of Africa, Asia, and Latin America where disease surveillance is limited.

### Causes

Pullorum disease is caused by the bacterium *Salmonella pullorum*. The disease spreads through:

- **Vertical transmission (Most Important)** — infected eggs from carrier hens; bacteria in egg yolk and albumen
- **Direct contact** — infected chick faeces contaminate environment
- **Contaminated feed and water** — chicks ingest bacteria
- **Mechanical transmission** — contaminated equipment, shoes, clothing
- **Horizontal transmission** — rare; less important than vertical transmission

Infected hens become lifelong carriers; they shed bacteria in eggs intermittently, resulting in infected chicks even from apparently healthy-looking eggs. Horizontal transmission among chicks is possible but less frequent.

## Symptoms

Symptoms appear within 24–48 hours of hatch and are most severe in first 1–2 weeks of life.

### In Young Chicks (< 2 weeks)

- **White diarrhoea** — chalky, white droppings; sticking to vent and feathers
- **Severe depression** — chicks huddled; eyes closed; ruffled feathers
- **High fever** — chicks feel hot to touch
- **Reduced feeding** — complete loss of appetite
- **Dehydration** — rapid weight loss; sunken abdomen
- **Huddling** — under heat source; reluctance to move
- **Gasping** — in severe cases; respiratory involvement
- **Death** — within 3–7 days if untreated
- **Mortality rate** — 50–90% of infected chicks; very high

### In Older Chicks (2–8 weeks)

- **Milder signs** — white diarrhoea; reduced appetite; depression
- **Poor growth** — infected chicks lag behind healthy flock-mates
- **Lower mortality** — 10–30% if treated; higher if untreated
- **Lameness** — if arthritis develops

### In Adult Birds (Carriers)

- **Asymptomatic** — most carrier birds show no signs
- **Chronic arthritis** — occasionally in long-term infected birds
- **Reduced egg production** — in carrier hens
- **Intermittent fever** — subclinical; not obvious without thermometer

## Diagnosis

### On-Farm Diagnosis

Based on clinical signs:

- White diarrhoea in chicks < 1 week of age
- High mortality in newly hatched chicks
- Depression and reduced appetite
- Multiple chicks affected simultaneously
- Flock history: infected hatching eggs or carrier birds

### Veterinary Diagnosis

A veterinarian confirms diagnosis by:

- **Bacterial culture** — faeces, blood, or organ cultures from affected chicks; *Salmonella pullorum* grows on selective media (e.g., Salmonella-Shigella agar)
- **Serology** — blood test (rapid test available); detects antibodies to *Salmonella pullorum*
- **PCR** — detects bacterial DNA from swabs or tissue
- **Necropsy** — chicks show septicaemia; enlarged spleen and liver; inflammation of intestines

## Prevention

### Source of Chicks

**Most Important Preventive Measure:**

1. **Buy chicks from pullorum-tested and pullorum-free breeder flocks** — verify test results
2. **Avoid chicks from unknown or untested sources** — high disease risk
3. **Quarantine chicks** — separate new chicks from existing flocks for 2–3 weeks
4. **Request disease status documentation** — from hatchery; verify breeder flock test results

### Breeder Flock Management

If keeping a breeding flock:

1. **Test breeder birds** — blood test all breeding hens for *Salmonella pullorum* serology
2. **Remove test-positive birds** — cull infected hens immediately
3. **Test regularly** — repeat testing every 6–12 months
4. **Maintain records** — document all test results
5. **Isolate new breeding birds** — quarantine for 2–3 weeks; test before adding to breeding flock

### Biosecurity for Chicks

1. **Clean and disinfect** — all equipment before receiving chicks
2. **Isolation** — house chicks separate from adult birds
3. **Staff hygiene** — dedicated staff for chicks; do not share equipment with adult flocks
4. **Disinfect feeders and drinkers** — daily; prevent faecal contamination
5. **Remove soiled litter** — frequently; keep environment dry
6. **Avoid cross-contamination** — do not move equipment between chick and adult flocks

## Treatment

### Antimicrobial Treatment

A veterinarian selects antimicrobials based on susceptibility testing. Early treatment critical; start within 12 hours of symptom onset.

| Antimicrobial | Route | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tetracycline | Feed or water | 5–7 days | First-line; broad-spectrum |
| Ampicillin/Amoxicillin | Water or injection | 3–5 days | Effective; injection expensive |
| Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole | Water or feed | 5–7 days | Broad-spectrum |
| Enrofloxacin | Water | 3–5 days | Fluoroquinolone; effective |

**Important:** Treat entire flock if disease confirmed; individual treatment ineffective. Isolate infected chicks if possible; treat separately to reduce environmental contamination.

### Supportive Care

1. **Water and electrolytes** — essential; provide clean water with electrolyte solution
2. **Soft feed** — moist, easily consumed feed
3. **Warmth** — maintain optimal temperature (32–35 °C for day-old chicks; gradually reduce)
4. **Dry environment** — remove soiled litter; keep bedding dry
5. **Good ventilation** — fresh air without drafts
6. **Monitoring** — check chicks frequently; remove dead and severely affected birds

### Recovery Indicators

- Appetite returns (day 1–2)
- White diarrhoea resolves; droppings normalize (day 3–5)
- Activity increases (day 2–3)
- Mortality decreases significantly (day 1–2 of treatment)
- Complete recovery takes 1–2 weeks

## Management

### If Pullorum Disease Occurs

1. **Confirm diagnosis** — contact veterinarian; blood test all chicks
2. **Initiate treatment** — start antimicrobial immediately on veterinarian's advice
3. **Isolate flock** — do not mix with other chicks or adult birds
4. **Clean and disinfect** — all equipment; change litter frequently
5. **Improve conditions** — ensure dry environment, good ventilation
6. **Monitor all birds** — check daily for new cases
7. **Record source** — identify hatchery or supplier; report disease
8. **Continue treatment** — do not stop early; complete full course

### If From Breeder Flock (Vertical Transmission)

1. **Test all breeder birds** — blood test entire flock
2. **Remove test-positive birds** — cull immediately
3. **Disinfect breeder housing** — thoroughly clean and spray
4. **Test again** — 2–3 weeks after removing positive birds; repeat until all tests negative
5. **Monitor eggs** — watch for disease in next chick batch; continue testing

### Post-Outbreak

1. **Quarantine surviving chicks** — separate for 2–3 weeks; monitor for recurrence
2. **Clean and disinfect** — all equipment; spray housing
3. **Restart flock** — only if confident breeder flock is pullorum-free
4. **Implement testing** — regular surveillance of breeder flock

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Contact a veterinarian if you observe:

- White diarrhoea in newly hatched chicks
- Multiple chicks dying within first week
- High mortality (> 10%) in chick shipment
- Chicks showing severe depression and fever
- Uncertain about chick source or disease status
- Need for pullorum testing of breeder flock
- Interest in disease prevention program

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any antimicrobial drug. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If pullorum disease outbreak suspected:

1. Isolate affected chicks immediately from other flocks
2. Contact veterinarian urgently for diagnosis confirmation
3. Start antimicrobial treatment on veterinarian's advice
4. Disinfect all equipment and housing
5. Do not sell or trade infected chicks
6. Do not move chicks to other farms
7. Report to veterinary authorities if suspected (disease surveillance)

## Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist for pullorum disease prevention and management:

- [ ] Chicks sourced from pullorum-tested, pullorum-free breeder flock
- [ ] Disease status documentation obtained from hatchery; filed and verified
- [ ] Chick housing cleaned and disinfected before chicks arrive
- [ ] Chicks isolated from adult birds; separate housing maintained
- [ ] Feeders and drinkers disinfected daily
- [ ] Soiled litter removed frequently; environment kept dry
- [ ] Clean water with electrolytes provided
- [ ] Staff dedicated to chicks; no sharing of equipment with adult flocks
- [ ] Chicks monitored daily for signs of disease
- [ ] If disease suspected: veterinarian contacted; diagnosis confirmed
- [ ] Antimicrobial prescribed and administered on schedule
- [ ] All affected chicks treated (flock treatment; not individual)
- [ ] Treatment continued for full prescribed duration
- [ ] Breeder flock tested regularly (if maintaining breeding birds)
- [ ] Test-positive birds removed from breeder flock immediately
- [ ] All test results recorded and filed
