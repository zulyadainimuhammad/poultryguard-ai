# Broiler Nutrition

## Overview

Broiler nutrition is the planned supply of balanced feed so meat birds grow quickly, remain healthy, and reach market weight on schedule. Good broiler diets balance energy, protein, amino acids, minerals, vitamins, and clean water across starter, grower, and finisher phases. Poor diet balance reduces growth, worsens feed conversion, and increases mortality from weakness and secondary disease. Practical feeding management is essential for profitable broiler production in low-resource and commercial systems.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Feed is usually the largest cost in broiler farming. Small nutrition errors can cause large losses through slower growth and poor feed conversion. Correct feeding improves immunity, leg strength, carcass quality, and flock uniformity. Better nutrition also reduces avoidable medicine use by supporting strong daily health.

### Nutrient Planning by Phase

Broiler needs change with age, so feed formulation should follow growth phases.

| Phase | Typical Age | Nutrition Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Starter | day 1 to 14 | Higher protein and balanced amino acids for early tissue growth |
| Grower | day 15 to 28 | Balanced energy and protein for efficient muscle development |
| Finisher | day 29 to market | Efficient energy use, controlled protein cost, and final weight gain |

### Causes of Poor Nutrition

- Low-quality or adulterated feed ingredients
- Incorrect feed formulation for bird age
- Sudden feed changes without transition
- Poor feeder space causing uneven intake
- Inadequate access to clean drinking water

### Risk Factors

- Long feed storage under hot and humid conditions
- Delayed feed delivery and inconsistent feeding times
- Overcrowding that reduces feeder access
- Lack of weighing and growth monitoring
- Reusing old feed with unknown quality

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Slow growth and low final body weight
- Poor feed conversion ratio and higher feed cost
- Uneven flock size and increased culling
- Weak legs, poor mobility, and reduced welfare
- Higher mortality during stress periods

### Prevention

Use age-appropriate feed phases and maintain steady feed availability. Source feed from trusted suppliers and protect feed from moisture and contamination. Provide enough feeder and drinker space for all birds. Transition diets gradually across one to two days to reduce digestive stress.

### Monitoring

Track nutrition performance each day and correct early deviations quickly.

- Weigh a sample of birds weekly to compare with targets.
- Record daily feed intake and estimate feed conversion trend.
- Inspect feeders for empty periods or excessive wastage.
- Check droppings and litter condition for digestive problems.
- Review mortality and cull reasons by age group.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Use starter, grower, and finisher feed in the correct age sequence.
2. Keep feed available at all times and avoid long feeder empty periods.
3. Ensure clean water is continuously available with good flow.
4. Buy feed ingredients from reliable and consistent sources.
5. Weigh birds weekly and adjust management when growth lags.
6. Reduce feed wastage by setting feeder height correctly.
7. Keep written records of feed use, growth, and mortality.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Act quickly when birds show sudden feed refusal, severe growth slowdown, or marked flock unevenness across multiple pens. Urgent review is needed when mortality rises with weak legs, dehydration, or severe weight loss. Immediate feed and water checks can prevent larger losses.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Correct feed phase is used for bird age
- [ ] Feed is available without long interruptions
- [ ] Clean water is continuously accessible
- [ ] Feed quality and storage conditions are checked
- [ ] Weekly bird weights are recorded
- [ ] Feed conversion trend is monitored
- [ ] Mortality and cull causes are reviewed weekly
