# Feed Storage

## Overview

Feed storage is the protection of poultry feed from moisture, heat, pests, and contamination before birds consume it. Good storage preserves nutrient quality, feed safety, and flock performance. Poor storage increases mold growth, mycotoxin risk, and nutrient loss, which can reduce growth and egg production. Practical storage control is a key part of feeding management and farm biosecurity.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Stored feed quality affects every bird each day. When feed quality falls, production losses can spread across the whole flock quickly. Safe storage reduces waste, lowers disease risk, and protects feed investment. Strong storage practice is especially important in hot and humid climates.

### Causes of Storage Failure

- Feed kept in damp rooms or on wet floors
- Poor ventilation in storage areas
- Torn bags and unsealed bulk containers
- Mixing old and new feed without stock rotation
- Delayed pest control around feed stores

### Risk Factors

- High ambient humidity and seasonal rains
- Long storage duration before feed use
- Lack of pallets and wall clearance for bag stacks
- Rodent and insect activity near stores
- Weak cleaning schedule for feed rooms and silos

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Reduced feed intake due to poor smell or taste
- Slower growth and poor feed conversion
- Egg production decline in layers
- Increased digestive disorders and uneven droppings
- Higher mortality when contamination is severe

### Prevention

Store feed in dry, clean, and well-ventilated rooms. Keep bags on pallets and away from walls to reduce moisture transfer and allow inspection. Use first-in, first-out stock rotation and mark all delivery dates. Protect feed stores from rodents, insects, and wild birds through regular sanitation and structural control.

### Monitoring

Check feed storage conditions daily and document all observations.

- Inspect feed for mold, caking, unusual smell, or color change.
- Measure room dryness and check for roof or wall leaks.
- Confirm stock rotation and removal of expired feed.
- Record pest activity and control actions.
- Track feed rejection, intake change, and production response.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Keep all feed off the floor using clean pallets.
2. Use first-in, first-out stock rotation without exception.
3. Seal feed rooms to reduce rodent and insect entry.
4. Remove spoiled or moldy feed immediately.
5. Clean storage areas and bins on a fixed schedule.
6. Buy feed quantities that match short, safe storage periods.
7. Keep records of deliveries, storage checks, and feed losses.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Respond immediately when feed develops moldy smell, visible fungal growth, or heavy pest infestation. Urgent action is also needed when birds suddenly refuse feed or show rapid production decline after a new feed batch. Quick isolation of suspect feed can prevent wider flock damage.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Feed is stored in a dry, ventilated, clean area
- [ ] Bags are stacked on pallets with wall clearance
- [ ] Stock rotation follows first-in, first-out
- [ ] Moldy, damaged, or expired feed is removed
- [ ] Rodent and insect control is active
- [ ] Storage area leaks and dampness are checked
- [ ] Delivery and storage inspection records are updated
