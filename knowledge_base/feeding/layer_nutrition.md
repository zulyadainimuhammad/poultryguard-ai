# Layer Nutrition

## Overview

Layer nutrition is the management of feed and water so laying hens maintain high egg production, strong shell quality, and good body condition throughout the production cycle. Laying birds need balanced energy, protein, calcium, phosphorus, and vitamins every day. Inadequate nutrition causes egg drop, weak shells, and faster flock decline. Consistent layer feeding improves production stability, bird welfare, and farm profitability.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Laying hens have high nutrient demand because egg formation happens daily. Small calcium or energy deficits can quickly reduce egg numbers and shell strength. Correct nutrition supports reproductive health, immune function, and persistence of lay over time. Reliable feeding reduces costly production fluctuations.

### Nutrient Priorities

Layer feed programmes should match bird age, production stage, and flock condition.

| Stage | Main Nutrition Priority |
|---|---|
| Pre-lay | Prepare body reserves and skeletal calcium stores |
| Peak lay | Support high egg mass with balanced energy and amino acids |
| Mid to late lay | Maintain shell quality and body condition as birds age |

### Causes of Poor Nutrition

- Low calcium or poorly available calcium sources
- Incorrect feed phase for production stage
- Inadequate feed intake during hot weather
- Irregular feeding schedule and feed interruptions
- Poor water access that limits feed consumption

### Risk Factors

- Heat stress reducing appetite
- Overcrowding around feeders and drinkers
- Poor feed mixing and uneven nutrient distribution
- Storage conditions that degrade vitamin quality
- Lack of routine egg and body condition monitoring

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Drop in egg production rate
- Thin, soft, or cracked shells
- Increased floor eggs and egg breakage
- Weight loss and poor feather condition
- Higher culling and reduced flock persistence

### Prevention

Provide phase-appropriate layer diets and maintain regular feeding times. Ensure consistent calcium supply and adequate particle size for shell formation. Keep water systems clean and functional because reduced water intake lowers feed intake rapidly. Adjust management quickly during heat periods to protect appetite and egg output.

### Monitoring

Use daily and weekly records to detect nutrition problems early.

- Record daily egg count and compare with expected production.
- Monitor shell quality and breakage rate each day.
- Check feed intake and water intake trends.
- Weigh sample hens at regular intervals.
- Review culling, mortality, and health observations.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Use diets formulated for pre-lay, peak lay, and late lay stages.
2. Ensure continuous access to clean water at all times.
3. Maintain reliable calcium and phosphorus levels in layer feed.
4. Feed at consistent times each day to support stable intake.
5. Increase heat management when appetite declines in hot weather.
6. Track egg output and shell quality daily.
7. Correct feed quality issues immediately with supplier follow-up.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Take urgent action when egg production drops sharply within a few days, when shell defects increase across many birds, or when hens show rapid body weight loss. Immediate review is also needed if birds reduce feed and water intake at the same time. Early correction can prevent long-term production decline.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Correct feed phase is used for flock production stage
- [ ] Calcium and phosphorus supply is adequate
- [ ] Water lines are clean and working properly
- [ ] Daily egg count and shell quality are recorded
- [ ] Feed intake changes are reviewed quickly
- [ ] Heat-related appetite loss is managed early
- [ ] Body condition and culling trends are tracked
