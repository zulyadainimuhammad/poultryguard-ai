# Water Quality

## Overview

Water quality is the safety and suitability of drinking water for poultry health, feed intake, growth, and egg production. Birds usually consume more water than feed by weight, so poor water quality can reduce performance very quickly. Contaminated or poorly managed water increases disease risk and lowers flock productivity. Reliable clean water is a basic requirement for all poultry systems.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Water supports digestion, temperature control, nutrient transport, and egg formation. If water quality drops, birds reduce intake and feed use falls at the same time. Poor water can also carry pathogens and increase biofilm in drinker systems. Maintaining water quality protects both feeding efficiency and farm biosecurity.

### Causes of Poor Water Quality

- Microbial contamination from dirty tanks or pipes
- Sediment and organic matter in source water
- Inadequate cleaning of drinkers and water lines
- Poor chlorination or disinfection control
- Broken lines that allow contamination entry

### Risk Factors

- Use of untreated surface water sources
- Hot weather that accelerates bacterial growth
- Long water line systems without flushing routine
- Infrequent tank cleaning
- High stocking density with heavy drinker use

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Reduced feed intake and lower weight gain
- Drop in egg production and shell quality
- Wet litter from drinker leakage or poor regulation
- Increased enteric disease and mortality risk
- Poor response to vaccines or medications delivered in water

### Prevention

Use a protected water source and maintain routine sanitation from storage tank to drinker point. Flush lines regularly and clean drinkers to reduce biofilm build-up. Apply approved water treatment based on veterinary or technical guidance and verify dosing. Repair leaks quickly to protect litter quality and reduce pathogen spread.

### Monitoring

Monitor water quality and water system performance on a regular schedule.

- Check water smell, clarity, and visible contamination daily.
- Record daily flock water intake and compare with expected trend.
- Inspect tanks, lines, and nipples for dirt, leaks, and blockage.
- Schedule periodic water testing for microbial and mineral quality.
- Review litter wetness patterns for drinker management problems.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Provide clean drinking water continuously without long interruptions.
2. Flush and sanitize water lines on a planned schedule.
3. Clean storage tanks and drinkers regularly.
4. Test water quality periodically, especially after seasonal changes.
5. Maintain correct drinker height and pressure for bird age.
6. Repair leaks immediately to reduce wet litter and contamination.
7. Keep records of water intake, treatment, and maintenance.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Take immediate action when birds suddenly reduce water intake, when foul smell or visible contamination appears in drinkers, or when wet litter spreads rapidly across houses. Urgent correction is also needed if diarrhea and production decline appear together after water system changes. Early response can prevent flock-wide losses.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Drinking water is continuously available
- [ ] Tanks and drinkers are cleaned routinely
- [ ] Water lines are flushed and sanitized
- [ ] Water intake is recorded daily
- [ ] Leaks and pressure problems are corrected quickly
- [ ] Periodic water testing is completed
- [ ] Water treatment and maintenance logs are updated
