# Mortality Monitoring

## Overview

Mortality monitoring is the routine tracking and analysis of bird deaths to detect health and management problems early. Daily mortality counts, when compared with expected flock trends, provide one of the fastest warning signals on a poultry farm. Good monitoring helps you respond before losses spread across houses or age groups. Reliable mortality data also supports veterinary investigation, biosecurity response, and long-term production planning. Consistent mortality review helps you distinguish normal flock variation from dangerous outbreak patterns.

## Key Information

### Why It Matters

Sudden increases in mortality often appear before full clinical outbreaks are obvious. If you monitor mortality every day, you can trigger faster disease control and management corrections. Mortality trends also reveal hidden issues such as poor feed quality, water failure, ventilation breakdown, and handling stress. Early action lowers economic loss and improves flock welfare outcomes.

### Causes of Poor Mortality Monitoring

- Deaths counted irregularly or at inconsistent times
- No separation of mortality by house or flock age
- Dead birds removed without recording probable cause
- Missing baseline targets for normal mortality
- Delayed communication of abnormal mortality spikes

### Risk Factors

- High-density houses with limited staff oversight
- Multiple flocks managed without standardized reporting
- Hot weather, cold stress, or ventilation failures
- Incomplete vaccination or weak biosecurity control
- No post-mortem follow-up during abnormal losses

### Clinical or Production Impacts

- Late outbreak detection and wider flock spread
- Higher cumulative mortality across the cycle
- Reduced growth and production from unresolved causes
- Increased treatment and replacement costs
- Lower confidence in farm performance and planning

### Prevention

Set a fixed mortality counting schedule and record by house, age, and date. Compare daily values against expected targets for the specific production type. Investigate abnormal patterns immediately and involve veterinary support where needed. Link mortality records with feed, water, and environmental logs to identify root causes quickly.

### Monitoring

Use clear daily thresholds and weekly trend review for every flock.

- Count and record all deaths at least once daily.
- Calculate daily mortality rate as a percentage of birds at risk.
- Track cumulative mortality by production week.
- Classify deaths by likely cause where possible.
- Trigger review when mortality exceeds expected baseline.

## Recommendations

### Farmer Guidance

1. Count mortalities at the same time every day.
2. Record by house and flock age, not only farm total.
3. Calculate daily and cumulative mortality percentages.
4. Investigate spikes within 24 hours.
5. Submit representative birds for veterinary diagnosis when needed.
6. Review mortality together with feed, water, and climate records.
7. Adjust biosecurity and management based on findings.

### Emergency Warning Signs

Treat as an emergency when daily mortality rises sharply above normal baseline, when multiple houses are affected at once, or when deaths cluster with respiratory or neurological signs. Immediate containment is required when mortality doubles over a short period without clear explanation. Rapid reporting and veterinary support can reduce wider losses.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Daily mortality count is completed on schedule
- [ ] Mortality is recorded by house and flock age
- [ ] Daily mortality rate is calculated
- [ ] Cumulative mortality trend is updated weekly
- [ ] Abnormal increases are reported immediately
- [ ] Diagnostic follow-up is initiated when required
- [ ] Corrective actions and outcomes are documented
