# Vaccine Storage and Handling

## Overview

Vaccine potency (strength and effectiveness) is destroyed by improper storage. Even one day of incorrect temperature ruins a batch. Poor storage is a common reason vaccines fail despite correct administration. Vaccines are living organisms (live vaccines) or sensitive biologics (inactivated vaccines) that require precise conditions. This document explains temperature requirements, storage equipment, monitoring, and practical solutions for smallholder farms with limited refrigeration.

## Key Information

### Temperature Requirements

**Live Vaccines (Newcastle, Gumboro, Bronchitis live strains):**
- Optimal storage: 2 to 8 °C (35 to 46 °F)
- Maximum temperature: 8 °C (46 °F)
- Minimum temperature: 2 °C (35 °F)
- Freezing destroys vaccine — never freeze
- Exceeding 8 °C for > 1 hour reduces potency
- Shelf life: 12–24 months at 2–8 °C (check label)

**Inactivated Vaccines (Oil-emulsion Newcastle, Bronchitis, Salmonella):**
- Optimal storage: 2 to 8 °C (35 to 46 °F)
- Temperature tolerance same as live vaccines
- Shelf life: 12–36 months at 2–8 °C (check label)
- Freezing acceptable for inactivated vaccines (may slightly alter emulsion)

**All Vaccines (General Rule):**
- Store at 2 to 8 °C
- Never expose to direct sunlight
- Never freeze live vaccines
- Discard vaccines left at room temperature > 4 hours
- Discard vaccines exposed to freezing (live only)

### Storage Equipment

**Refrigerator (Best Option):**
- Dedicated vaccine refrigerator is ideal
- Do not store food or other items (risk of contamination)
- Place thermometer inside; check daily (write readings)
- Ensure airflow around vaccine boxes; do not overcrowd
- Keep freezer compartment shut (do not store vaccines in freezer)
- Back-up power (solar panel, generator, battery) essential in areas with power cuts

**Cooler Box with Ice Packs (Field Use):**
- Use high-quality insulated cooler (foam or plastic)
- Place ice packs on bottom and sides; never in direct contact with vaccines
- Separate ice from vaccines with cloth barrier
- Place vaccines in middle of cooler
- Keep lid closed until vaccination time
- Temperature typically stays 2–8 °C for 4–6 hours
- Monitor with thermometer; discard if exceeds 8 °C

**Cold Chain (Multi-level System):**
- District-level: walk-in cold room or large refrigerator
- Regional hubs: vaccine refrigerators with backup power
- Farm level: small refrigerator or cooler boxes for transport
- Each level monitors temperature and documents it

### Proper Vaccine Handling

**At Purchase:**

1. Check expiration date on label — do not buy expired vaccines
2. Verify temperature history — ask supplier if kept cold
3. Inspect vials for cracks, leaks, discoloration
4. Ask for batch number and expiration date documentation
5. Transport in cooler box with ice packs
6. Refrigerate immediately upon arrival

**Before Opening:**

1. Read label carefully — vaccine name, expiration date, storage requirement, contraindications
2. Inspect for damage — cracks, leaks, discoloration, frozen appearance
3. Check reconstitution instructions (if lyophilised/freeze-dried)
4. Discard if reconstitution diluent appears contaminated
5. Use within indicated time after opening (typically 1‒2 hours)

**After Opening:**

1. Use immediately according to mixing instructions
2. Do not store reconstituted vaccine
3. Any unused vaccine opened > 2 hours must be discarded
4. Do not combine partial vials with remaining vaccine
5. Properly dispose of opened vials and syringes (bury or incinerate)

### Vaccine Shelf Life and Expiration

| Vaccine Type | Shelf Life | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Live Newcastle (freeze-dried) | 12–24 months | Check specific product; shorter in tropical climates |
| Inactivated Newcastle (oil-emulsion) | 12–36 months | Longer shelf life than live |
| Gumboro live | 12–18 months | Freeze-dried; sensitive to temperature |
| Bronchitis live | 12–18 months | Freeze-dried; sensitive to temperature |
| Marek's vaccine (hatchery) | Varies | Typically 12 months; not stored on farms |

**Never use expired vaccines.** Expired vaccines may be ineffective and provide false sense of security.

### Temperature Monitoring

**Daily Monitoring:**

1. Place alcohol thermometer inside refrigerator (not on door)
2. Check thermometer every morning and evening
3. Record readings in notebook: date, temperature, time of day
4. Alert veterinarian if temperature deviates > 1 °C from 2–8 °C range
5. Do not open refrigerator unnecessarily (temperature spike)

**Monthly Monitoring:**

1. Check vacuum seal (if applicable) — broken seal indicates temperature rise
2. Inspect vials for frosting or condensation (sign of freezing)
3. Check vaccine color — discoloration indicates potency loss
4. Verify expiration dates on all stored vaccines
5. Remove any expired vaccines

**Problem-Solving:**

- **Too warm (> 8 °C):** Check refrigerator function, ensure door seals properly, reduce ambient heat, add ice packs during day
- **Too cold (< 2 °C):** Remove ice packs, adjust thermostat, use cloth barrier between ice and vaccines
- **Power failure:** Use backup generator or cooler boxes with ice packs; transfer vaccines if outage > 2 hours

## Recommendations

Follow these steps for safe vaccine storage:

1. **Establish cold chain** — identify storage location (refrigerator or cooler) before ordering vaccines
2. **Arrange backup power** — solar panel, generator, battery, or UPS for refrigerator
3. **Label everything** — mark all stored vaccines with date received, expiration date
4. **Monitor daily** — check temperature every morning; record in notebook
5. **Order fresh vaccines** — buy only what will be used within shelf life
6. **Transport safely** — use insulated cooler with ice packs; never leave in sun
7. **Use oldest first** — apply FIFO (first in, first out) principle; rotate stock
8. **Train staff** — ensure all personnel understand temperature sensitivity
9. **Discard old stock** — remove expired vaccines; do not attempt to use
10. **Troubleshoot problems** — call veterinarian if temperature control failing

## When to Call a Veterinarian

Contact a veterinarian if:

- Refrigerator temperature exceeds 8 °C and cannot be corrected
- Vaccines have been left unrefrigerated > 4 hours
- Vaccine vials show signs of freezing or overheating
- Power failure lasting > 2 hours (vaccine viability uncertain)
- Expiration date approaching but vaccines still in stock
- Unsure if vaccines were stored correctly during transport

**Always consult a licensed veterinarian before administering any drug or vaccine. The guidance in this document is for general awareness only and does not replace professional veterinary advice.**

### Emergency Actions

If cold chain is broken:

1. Stop vaccination immediately
2. Move vaccines to properly functioning refrigerator
3. Call veterinarian to assess vaccine viability
4. Do not use vaccines unless veterinarian confirms they are safe
5. If uncertain, discard vaccines and order fresh stock
6. Vaccinate animals only after new vaccines received and stored correctly

### Farmer Checklist

Use this checklist for vaccine storage:

- [ ] Dedicated vaccine refrigerator or cooler available
- [ ] Thermometer installed inside refrigerator; checked daily
- [ ] Temperature monitoring recorded in notebook (date, time, °C)
- [ ] Backup power arranged (solar, generator, or UPS)
- [ ] All vaccines labeled: received date, expiration date
- [ ] Vaccines stored at 2–8 °C continuously
- [ ] Freezer compartment remains shut; vaccines never placed there
- [ ] No food or other items stored with vaccines
- [ ] Refrigerator door seals properly; opens/closes smoothly
- [ ] Expired vaccines removed and disposed of safely
- [ ] Vaccines rotated: oldest used first
- [ ] Cooler box and ice packs available for transport
- [ ] Ice packs separated from vaccines by cloth barrier
- [ ] Thermometer in cooler box; temperature checked after transport
- [ ] Staff trained on vaccine storage requirements
